The Problem

HBM is not just small. It gets chopped into unusable islands.

Large-model inference mixes long-lived KV caches and expert weights with short-lived activation buffers. Even when total free HBM looks healthy, the largest free extent can collapse until a large tensor no longer fits contiguously, forcing faults, migrations, or fallback behavior.

Fragmented HBM

Why it matters

  • Faults increase because reusable tensors are no longer resident when needed.
  • Allocator pressure rises because free HBM is scattered across many holes.
  • Fallback epochs multiply, shutting down discretionary actions exactly when pressure is highest.

How It Works

Forecast first. Act only when the evidence and the budget say it is safe.

The policy operates as a control plane above the allocator. It predicts reuse, gates admission and eviction with confidence bounds, tracks thrash debt, and only compacts when the runtime explicitly exposes a safe window.

1. Reuse Forecast

Every touch can carry a reuse estimate (`mu`) and uncertainty (`sigma`). The policy converts those into lower and upper confidence bounds instead of treating every forecast as equally trustworthy.

2. Confidence Gate

Admission uses the lower bound, so only strong candidates enter HBM. Eviction uses the upper bound, which adds hysteresis and reduces immediate thrash on noisy forecasts.

3. Thrash Budget Ledger

Faults and migration bytes are charged against a fixed per-epoch budget. Once the ledger is exhausted, the simulator flips into deterministic fallback and blocks discretionary actions.

4. Safe-Window Compaction

Compaction is only requested when fragmentation threatens upcoming allocations or when the free space becomes too scattered. Even then, it only runs inside explicitly marked safe windows.
